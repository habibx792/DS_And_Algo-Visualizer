Arrays form one of the most fundamental structures in computer science. You can think of them as a row of neatly arranged storage boxes, each holding a value and each reachable by its position number, known as the index. Because all the elements sit right next to each other in memory, accessing any item is incredibly fastâ€”almost instant. Creating an array is straightforward: decide how many items you want to store, choose their type, and place them side by side. Once created, arrays shine when you need quick lookups, predictable order, and efficient iteration. To implement arrays well, treat them with intention. Use clear indexing, avoid going out of bounds, and choose sizes that reflect how the data will grow. When flexibility is needed, dynamic structures like lists or vectors can step in, but arrays remain the ideal choice whenever speed and structure matter most.
